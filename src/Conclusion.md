This exploratory data analysis aimed to identify which demographic, lifestyle, and clinical factors are associated with a higher risk of developing coronary heart disease (CHD) within the next 10 years.

The analysis showed that age is one of the strongest predictors of CHD. Older patients were significantly more likely to develop coronary heart disease, which confirms the idea that cardiovascular risk increases with aging.

Smoking also showed an important relationship with CHD. Patients who smoked more cigarettes per day, especially moderate and heavy smokers, presented a higher probability of developing CHD. This supports the hypothesis that smoking is a major cardiovascular risk factor.

Hypertension was another key variable. Patients with high systolic blood pressure and diagnosed hypertension (prevalentHyp) showed a much higher CHD prevalence. The distribution plots and multivariate analysis clearly confirmed this relationship.

Diabetes and glucose levels were also associated with increased cardiovascular risk. Patients with diabetes and higher glucose values were more likely to develop CHD, although this effect was slightly less pronounced than hypertension.

Total cholesterol also contributed to CHD risk, but its impact appeared more moderate compared to age and blood pressure.

BMI showed a weaker relationship than expected. While obesity may contribute to cardiovascular risk, it was not among the strongest predictors in this dataset.

Gender differences were also observed, with male patients showing a slightly higher prevalence of CHD compared to female patients.

Overall, the multivariate analysis confirmed that the highest-risk profile is typically an older male patient who smokes, has hypertension, elevated glucose levels, and higher cholesterol.

The initial hypotheses were mostly validated. Age, smoking, hypertension, diabetes, and cholesterol were confirmed as important risk factors, while BMI showed a weaker influence than initially expected.

In conclusion, the Framingham dataset confirms that coronary heart disease is mainly associated with a combination of demographic factors, unhealthy lifestyle habits, and clinical conditions, especially age, smoking, and blood pressure.
